## Overview

Precognition (Latin prae-, 'before' + cognitio, 'acquiring knowledge') is the alleged paranormal ability to perceive future events directly. It is a subtype of extrasensory perception (ESP) studied historically within parapsychology, particularly through forced-choice card tests at J. B. Rhine's Duke University laboratory from the 1930s. Notable later work includes Helmut Schmidt's random-event-generator experiments in the 1960s and Daryl Bem's nine-experiment 'Feeling the Future' paper in the Journal of Personality and Social Psychology (2011, doi:10.1037/a0021524). Bem reported small effects consistent with precognition; pre-registered replications by Ritchie, Wiseman, and French (2012, PLOS ONE) and a large 2023 multi-lab pre-registered study failed to find an effect. Samuel Soal's 1940s ESP experiments, originally cited as supportive evidence, were shown by Betty Markwick (1978) to involve data manipulation. Mainstream scientific bodies and the broader psychological community treat precognition as pseudoscience: it conflicts with the causal direction of time in physics, has not produced replicable effects, and is plausibly explained by coincidence, confirmation bias, false memory, retrofitted prediction, and selection effects.

## Notable Sightings

### Rhine ESP card experiments at Duke ⭐
> **Date**: 1934-01-01  
> **Location**: Duke University, Durham, North Carolina  
J. B. Rhine and Louisa Rhine's Duke laboratory ran large-scale Zener-card experiments testing telepathy, clairvoyance, and (especially after 1938) precognition. Rhine's 1934 monograph reported above-chance scoring rates and helped establish parapsychology as a distinct field, though independent re-examinations identified inadequate randomisation and sensory leakage in many tasks.
**Credibility**: low — Foundational case in the history of psi research; not regarded today as evidence for genuine precognition.
**Sources**: Rhine, J. B. (1934), Extra-Sensory Perception; Wikipedia: Joseph Banks Rhine

---

### Bem 'Feeling the Future' nine experiments ⭐
> **Date**: 2011-03-01  
> **Location**: Cornell University, Ithaca, New York  
Daryl Bem's nine-experiment paper reported small but statistically significant effects consistent with precognition across protocols including 'precognitive detection of erotic stimuli' and 'retroactive practice'. Pre-registered independent replications (Ritchie, Wiseman & French 2012; Galak et al. 2012; 2023 multi-lab) failed to find the effects. The paper is widely cited as a catalyst for psychology's replication-crisis reforms.
**Credibility**: low — Best-known modern laboratory precognition study; null on replication.
**Sources**: Bem, D. J. (2011), JPSP doi:10.1037/a0021524; Ritchie, S. J., Wiseman, R., & French, C. C. (2012), PLOS ONE 7(3): e33423

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Genuine precognitive psi** _(J. B. Rhine, Daryl Bem, Dean Radin, Helmut Schmidt)_

Mind has a real, if weak, capacity to access information from future events; a class of 'anomalous cognition' to be characterised by parapsychology.

**Retrocausal / quantum-influence model** _(Some parapsychology theorists)_

Speculative interpretations invoke retrocausality, quantum entanglement, or block-universe time to argue precognition is compatible with physics.

### Skeptical

**Failed replication and pseudoscience classification** _(Richard Wiseman, Chris French, James Alcock, Stuart Ritchie)_

Mainstream scientific consensus, supported by repeated failed pre-registered replications, treats precognition as a pseudoscience.

**Cognitive and statistical artefacts** _(James Randi, Susan Blackmore, Ray Hyman)_

Apparent precognitive experiences are produced by coincidence, confirmation bias, hindsight bias, false memory, retrofitting predictions to events, and selection effects in spontaneous cases; lab effects by underpowered statistics, optional stopping, and questionable research practices.

**Causality conflict** _(Mainstream physics commentators)_

Effects that precede their causes contradict the causal direction of time established in physics; absent extraordinary evidence, mundane explanations are preferred.
