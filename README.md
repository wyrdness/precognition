## Description

Precognition is the purported paranormal ability to perceive or gain knowledge of future events before they occur, without using any known sensory or logical processes. The term derives from Latin *prae* (before) and *cognitio* (acquiring knowledge), literally meaning "foreknowledge." Also called premonition, prescience, future sight, or second sight, precognition represents one of the most philosophically challenging paranormal claims due to its implications for causality, determinism, and the nature of time itself.

Precognitive experiences range from vague feelings of unease before disasters to specific, detailed visions of future events. They may occur in dreams, waking visions, sudden intuitions, or through deliberate divination practices. While widespread in human culture and reported across all societies, precognition remains scientifically unverified and raises fundamental questions about the relationship between consciousness, time, and physical reality.

## Types and Forms

### Spontaneous Precognition

**Dream Precognition:**
- Most commonly reported form
- Vivid dreams of future events
- Often emotionally significant events
- May be symbolic or literal
- Example: Aberfan disaster premonitions (1966)
- Difficult to distinguish from coincidence

**Waking Premonitions:**
- Sudden knowledge of future events
- "Bad feeling" about specific situations
- Intuitive warnings
- Sometimes accompanied by physical sensations
- Anxiety, nausea, or dread

**Crisis Premonitions:**
- Foreknowledge of accidents, deaths, disasters
- Often involves loved ones
- Strong emotional component
- May feel compulsive or urgent
- Retrospectively validated (claimed)

**Dé jà Vu:**
- Feeling of having experienced situation before
- Could be interpreted as precognitive memory
- More likely neurological phenomenon
- Very common experience
- Usually brief and vague

### Experimental Precognition

**Laboratory Testing:**
- Guessing future random events
- Card prediction tests
- Random number generator predictions
- Brain activity before stimuli (presentiment)
- Controversial results

**Presentiment Studies:**
- Physiological arousal before random emotional images
- Dean Radin, Dick Bierman research
- Claims of statistical significance
- Replication issues
- Highly controversial

**Precognitive Remote Viewing:**
- Describing future target locations
- Used in military experiments
- Star Gate Project included precognition
- Mixed and disputed results

### Cultural Divination Practices

- Delphi Oracle (ancient Greece)

- Astrology

**Prophetic Traditions:**

- Meta-analyses showing small effects

- All highly controversial

### The Daryl Bem Controversy (2011)

**Study Claims:**
- Nine experiments showing precognition
- "Feeling the Future" paper
- Published in *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*
- Reversed causality experiments
- Statistically significant results claimed

**Criticisms:**
- Questionable statistical methods
- Multiple comparisons problem
- Optional stopping
- File drawer effect
- Failure to replicate

**Impact:**
- Highlighted replication crisis in psychology
- Debates about statistical methods
- Pre-registration movement strengthened
- Increased skepticism of extraordinary claims

### Skeptical Analysis

**Methodological Issues:**
- Recording errors
- Sensory leakage
- Statistical artifacts
- Confirmation bias
- Selective reporting

**Cognitive Explanations:**
- Memory reconstruction
- False memories
- Coincidence
- Probability misunderstanding
- Pattern recognition overactivity

**Replication Failure:**
- Independent replications usually fail
- "Experimenter effects" claimed but problematic
- No reliable protocol established
- Decline effect in parapsychology
- Should precognition predict replication failures?

## Psychological Explanations

### Cognitive Biases

**Confirmation Bias:**
- Remember "hits," forget "misses"
- Selective attention to confirming evidence
- Discount contradictory information
- Strengthen belief over time

**Hindsight Bias:**
- "I knew it all along"
- Memory reconstruction
- Retrofitting knowledge
- False sense of prediction

**Availability Heuristic:**
- Dramatic cases more memorable
- Overestimate frequency
- Statistical reasoning failure
- Media amplification effect

**Apophenia:**
- Pattern recognition in randomness
- Seeing connections that don't exist
- Evolutionary bias toward false positives
- Meaningful coincidences

### Memory Processes

**False Memories:**
- Memory is reconstructive, not reproductive
- Details added post-event
- Confidence doesn't equal accuracy
- Elizabeth Loftus research
- Suggestion and social pressure

**Source Monitoring:**
- Confusing imagination with memory
- "Did I dream it or experience it?"
- Source confusion common
- Vivid imagination encoded as memory

### Dream Psychology

**Dream Interpretation:**
- Symbols can fit many outcomes
- Vague enough to match events
- Emotional processing function
- Not prophetic
- Freudian and Jungian theories

**Anxiety Dreams:**
- Reflect current worries
- May "come true" if worry realistic
- Coincidence, not precognition
- Preparation function

**Dream Recall:**
- Selective and reconstructive
- Dreams forgotten unless written down
- Post-event memory contamination
- Confirmation bias in recall

## Famous Cases and Claims

### Aberfan Disaster (1966)

**Event:**
- Coal waste tip collapsed on Welsh village
- 144 killed, mostly children
- School buried
- October 21, 1966

**Claimed Premonitions:**
- Dr. J.C. Barker collected accounts
- 76 claimed precognitive experiences
- Dreams of schools, children, blackness
- Most reported after event
- Retrospective bias likely
- No verified pre-disaster documentation

**Skeptical Analysis:**
- Self-reporting after tragedy
- Memory reconstruction
- Emotional desire to give meaning
- No control group (how many had unrelated dreams?)
- Statistical inevitability given population size

### Titanic Disaster (1912)

**Claims:**
- Various people said they canceled passage due to premonition
- Dreams and bad feelings reported
- Some documented before sinking
- W.T. Stead's supposed premonition

**Alternative Explanations:**
- "Unsinkable" ship invited anxiety
- Rational concerns about maiden voyage
- Confirmation bias (many didn't cancel)
- Stories embellished over time
- No systematic documentation

### Lincoln Assassination Premonitions

**Lincoln's Dream:**
- Reportedly dreamed of his death

- Documented after assassination
- May be apocryphal or embellished
- Anxiety dreams during war common

## Conclusion

Precognition remains one of the most intriguing and problematic paranormal claims. Personal experiences of apparent future knowledge are common and can be subjectively compelling, but systematic investigation has failed to produce credible, replicable evidence for genuine precognition. The phenomenon is better explained through well-understood cognitive processes: pattern recognition, memory reconstruction, probability misunderstanding, and various cognitive biases.

The theoretical problems with precognition are profound. Any mechanism for genuinely knowing the future would require revolutionary changes to our understanding of time, causality, and consciousness—changes unsupported by current physics or neuroscience. The logical paradoxes involved in precognition suggest fundamental incoherence in the concept itself.

For those who have experienced apparent premonitions, it's worth considering that humans possess remarkable, scientifically explicable abilities to anticipate the future through learning, pattern recognition, and unconscious processing. These natural capabilities are impressive without requiring paranormal explanation. The sense of having "known" something in advance likely reflects the reconstructive nature of memory and the biases that help us find meaning in our experiences.

Understanding why precognition beliefs persist—and why experiences of precognition feel so convincing—offers valuable insights into human cognition, the nature of consciousness, and our relationship with time and uncertainty.
